# NY Racing — Sponsorship ROI Case Study (Project 1, Week 1)

**[Mission:]** Prove that sponsor visibility has measurable value. NY Racing — a Black-owned, part-time NASCAR Cup team with an HBCU-focused mission — must give its primary sponsor data-driven evidence of return on investment in order to retain the partnership, since 60–80% of a team's operating budget comes from sponsorship. The work done here becomes the foundation for a visibility scoring model that quantifies exposure even across a limited race schedule. Everything connects back to one idea: turning visibility into a defensible number.

**[Problem:]** A mid-tier team's primary sponsor is questioning whether its multi-million-dollar investment is worth it, and prize money alone cannot sustain the team (winning the Daytona 500 pays only ~$2.5M against $20–35M annual budgets). Proving ROI is hard because the value is fragmented across broadcast, social, and in-venue exposure, and the biggest returns (affinity, trust, loyalty) are the hardest to quantify. As a part-time team with fewer races and lower on-track media value, NY Racing is hit harder by every measurement challenge than a full-time, championship-contending operation.

---

**[Sponsor Challenges]**

- **Attribution is nearly impossible.** A logo on a car does not come with a tracked conversion, so isolating the sponsorship's effect on sales from pricing, other advertising, seasonality, and economic conditions cannot be done cleanly. This is the deepest ROI problem in the industry and the one least likely to be solved for a small team. The honest position is to prove value delivered (exposure, engagement, audience quality) rather than claim causation of sales.

- **The most valuable returns are intangible.** The bulk of sponsorship value — brand affinity, trust, reputation, and loyalty — is exactly the part that is hardest to measure, so it gets under-counted or hand-waved. Research cited across the industry suggests over 70% of NASCAR fans choose sponsor brands over non-sponsors, but that is directional, not causal proof. Capturing these soft returns credibly is the difference between a deal that renews and one that gets cut.

- **Data is fragmented and standards are inconsistent.** Exposure lives across broadcast, social media, and in-venue activations in separate systems with different units, making it hard to unify into a single ROI figure. Different measurement vendors (Joyce Julius, Nielsen, Zoomph) use different methodologies and rate cards, so "media value" is not comparable across providers. A skeptical CFO can always question the methodology behind a headline number.

- **Value is performance-dependent and volatile.** A primary Cup sponsor's equivalent media value ranges from roughly $5M to $15M per season, and that figure swings with on-track results, crashes, and broadcast airtime. A bad-luck season can tank the headline number through no fault of the sponsorship itself. For a part-time team running fewer races, the smaller sample size amplifies this variance and weakens any single-number argument.

---

**[Proxy Metrics]**

- **Quality-adjusted exposure value.** Rather than raw impression counts (a vanity metric), measure equivalent media value weighted by logo clarity, size, on-screen duration, and centrality. AI computer-vision tools now score these quality factors automatically across broadcast and social footage. Reporting quality-adjusted impressions gives a far more defensible figure than gross exposure that says nothing about whether anyone actually noticed the brand.

- **Per-race and per-dollar efficiency.** Calculate value-per-race weighted by event audience size (Daytona and Talladega draw six to nine million viewers versus far smaller mid-season races) and value-per-dollar-spent. These normalized metrics let a part-time team compete on efficiency even when it loses on total season exposure. Because the modern market increasingly buys sponsorships per-race (deals of 3, 6, or 11 races are now common), a focused part-time schedule reads as a natural, premium inventory rather than a deficiency.

- **Earned and social media value.** Track owned and earned social posts, engagement rate, video views, share of voice, and follower growth tied to the partnership. This value is schedule-independent — it compounds between races — which partially neutralizes the part-time disadvantage. It is also where a compelling team story generates amplification that on-car placement alone never could.

- **Audience composition, affinity, and sentiment.** Measure demographics, regional skew, brand affinity, and especially the share of audience matching the sponsor's actual target market. A smaller, on-target audience can be worth more per impression than a large generic one, which reframes a niche team as a precision buy. Sentiment analysis adds a layer that captures whether the brand-team association is landing positively — both an upside metric and a risk flag for mission-driven deals.

- **Hospitality, B2B access, and recall lift.** Count garage passes, VIP suite days, B2B meetings hosted, and deals influenced through the Thursday–Sunday race-weekend access that NASCAR uniquely offers. Layer in pre/post brand-recall and purchase-intent surveys among exposed fans as the cleanest available bridge toward sales impact. Together these proxies build a layered argument that gets as close to true ROI as the data honestly allows.

---

**[NY Racing Approach]**

- **Reframe the part-time schedule as a strength, not an apology.** Stop comparing NY Racing to full-time, championship-contending teams on total exposure — a comparison it cannot win. Instead, lead with weighted value-per-race and value-per-dollar at high-profile events where the team chooses to compete. The fractional, per-race buying model that now dominates the sport makes a focused part-time slate look like a clean premium inventory.

- **Monetize the mission through earned and audience-quality metrics.** NY Racing's Black ownership and HBCU initiative generate earned media, social engagement, and access to an audience NASCAR has historically failed to reach — value that pure logo-impression metrics miss entirely. Quantify this through audience composition, affinity, sentiment, and earned-media value rather than treating it as purely qualitative. This directly answers the demand from tech and fintech entrants who increasingly enter NASCAR through digital content and DEI-aligned partnerships.

- **Measure value delivered, concede causation openly.** Build the visibility model on the layered proxy stack — quality-adjusted exposure, efficiency, earned/social, audience quality, hospitality, and recall lift — while stating plainly where causal sales attribution ends. A model that knows its own limits earns more trust from a skeptical sponsor than one that overclaims and collapses under scrutiny at renewal. Conservative, well-sourced numbers are the strongest tool for keeping the sponsor signed.

- **Let the mission drive the questions, never the answers.** Caring about the team's social purpose is a reason to be more disciplined with the data, not less, because advocacy dressed up as analysis is easy for a finance team to detect and dismiss. The most valuable thing the analysis can do for John Cohen's mission is produce figures a hostile CFO cannot poke holes in. Source every key figure to named research, and present estimates conservatively rather than at the top of their plausible range.
