# NASCAR Sponsorship Case Comparison & NY Racing Action Plan

**Prepared for:** Sarah Mitchell, VP of Partnerships, NY Racing (No. 44)
**Purpose:** Compare four benchmark sponsorships across the case-study framework and ROI measurement methods, then define a defensible, data-driven visibility scoring model for NY Racing.

---

## Part 1 — Case Study Comparison

### The four cases at a glance

| Case | Verdict | Duration | Structure / Tier | One-line takeaway |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| M&Ms / Kyle Busch (Joe Gibbs Racing, #18) | Success, then clean exit | 2008–2022 (15 yrs) | Full-season primary (~$15–20M/yr) | Performance + elite activation drove a 15-year run; ended when the driver/team combo changed. |
| Ally Financial / Hendrick (Alex Bowman, #48) | Success | 2019–2026+ | Full/anchor primary | A mid-tier driver delivers strong ROI through measurement discipline and counterintuitive fit. |
| Dollar General (multiple teams) | Failure / exit | 2012–2017 | Primary | Declining activation + no agreed metrics + leadership change = exit as "unproven." |
| Bass Pro Shops / Austin Dillon (RCR, #3) | Success (loyalty/fit) | RCR since 1998; Dillon career-long | Partial-season anchor primary (12→17 races) | Authentic audience fit sustains a non-elite driver for decades via a hybrid anchor model. |

### Applying the case-study analysis framework

Scored High / Medium / Low against the five framework dimensions. These are analytical judgments drawn from the case material, not published scores.

| Dimension | M&Ms / Busch | Ally / Bowman | Dollar General | Bass Pro / Dillon |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Brand Fit** | High — family/impulse brand meets family NASCAR audience | Strategic — counterintuitive; targets "future customers" rivals ignore | High initially — working-class fit | High — outdoor/conservation maps almost perfectly onto NASCAR fans |
| **Driver Performance** | Outstanding — 56 wins, 2 titles during the deal | Solid, not elite — 7 wins, regular playoff contender | Variable — entered during performance uncertainty | Mid-pack — 5 career wins, occasional playoffs |
| **Activation Quality** | Industry-leading — characters, retail packaging, cross-promos (30–50% activation multiplier) | Exceptional & measurable — "#48 in 48," QR/app tracking, financial literacy | Declined over time — paid for placement, stopped activating | Values-led — Winchester co-brand, conservation campaigns, themed liveries |
| **Partnership Duration** | 15 years across three renewal cycles | 6+ years and growing | ~5 years, then exit | 25+ years with RCR; career-long with Dillon |
| **Measurable Outcomes** | Strong (renewals as proxy; no public ROI) | Publicly stated: +40% brand awareness, lower CAC than digital | ROI "hard to justify"; cut in budget squeeze | Renewal + race-count growth (12→17) as the hard signal |

### What separates success from failure

The cases converge on a consistent pattern. The winners share three traits the failure lacked: **authentic brand-audience fit, sustained activation investment beyond the logo, and a measurement habit that gave the sponsor a reason to renew.** On-track performance helps (M&Ms) but is not required (Ally, Bass Pro both succeeded with non-elite or mid-pack results). The failure case (Dollar General) shows the death spiral in reverse: activation spend declined, no success metrics had been agreed upfront, a new CMO favored channels with cleaner attribution, and in a budget squeeze the unmeasured sponsorship was cut first.

The single most important structural insight is that two of the highest-impact ROI factors — activation investment and measurement discipline — are fully controllable by the team and sponsor regardless of finishing position. A team cannot manufacture wins, but it can manufacture measurement and make activation easy.

## Part 2 — ROI Measurement Methods Comparison

The industry uses four main methods, each with different strengths and a different fit for a part-time team. NY Racing can only directly approximate two of them with public data.

| Method | What it measures | Strength | Limitation | Usable by NY Racing on public data? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Media Value Equivalency (MVE)** | Ad-cost equivalent of logo screen time | Concrete, dollar-denominated | Performance-dependent; methodologies differ by vendor | Partial — "MVE-lite" via finish position + airtime proxies |
| **Brand Lift / Awareness Studies** | Change in awareness, perception, purchase intent | Isolates sponsorship's perception impact | Requires costly surveys; sponsor-side data | No — needs sponsor cooperation |
| **Sales Correlation Analysis** | Sales in NASCAR markets vs. control markets | Closest to true ROI | Confounded; requires sponsor's private sales data | No — needs sponsor-side sales data |
| **Digital / Engagement Metrics** | Social mentions, video views, web traffic, sentiment | Real-time, directly measurable, public | Engagement ≠ purchase; sentiment can turn negative | **Yes — core of the model** |

**Implication:** NY Racing's defensible position is to build a model on the two methods it can actually evidence — a quality-adjusted MVE proxy and digital/engagement metrics — while being transparent that brand-lift and sales-correlation require sponsor-side data the team does not control. Honesty about that boundary is a credibility asset, not a weakness.

### How each case was measured (lesson for the model)

- **M&Ms:** Renewals and category dominance as proxies; no public ROI. *Lesson: longevity is evidence, but soft.*
- **Ally:** Rigorous, day-one tracking with stated outcomes (+40% awareness, lower CAC). *Lesson: this is the gold standard to emulate.*
- **Dollar General:** Effectively unmeasured; "couldn't justify." *Lesson: absence of measurement is itself the failure mode.*
- **Bass Pro:** Race-count growth as a spend-based signal of value. *Lesson: track the sponsor's own commitment trajectory as an outcome metric.*

## Part 3 — NY Racing Visibility Scoring Model: Plan of Action

### Design principle

The model does not claim to prove sales causation — no one in the industry can on public data. It proves **value delivered** (exposure, engagement, audience quality, sentiment) through publicly available, reproducible proxy metrics, normalized for a part-time schedule. The goal is to be the team that solved the measurement problem first, making NY Racing the rational long-term anchor bet in an era of sponsor churn.

### Step 1 — Define the sponsor's objective before measuring

Every pitch begins by identifying whether the prospective sponsor wants **acquisition** (like Ally) or **loyalty/brand identity** (like Bass Pro), because that determines which metrics constitute proof. The model must be configurable to weight metrics accordingly rather than reporting a single generic score.

### Step 2 — Assemble public proxy data sources

| Data source | What it measures | Access |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Race performance (finish, laps led, running position) | On-track / camera-time proxy | Public — Racing Reference, NASCAR.com |
| Reddit r/NASCAR mentions | Fan conversation volume & sentiment | Public API |
| YouTube engagement (views, likes, comments) | Video visibility & engagement | Public API |
| News mentions | Earned media coverage | Semi-public — manual / search |

### Step 3 — Build a weighted, quality-adjusted score

- **Quality-adjusted exposure**, not raw impressions — weight by logo clarity, on-screen duration, and event audience size, so a high-profile race (Daytona, Talladega) counts more than a mid-card event.
- **Value-per-race and value-per-dollar**, weighted by event audience — the metrics that let a part-time team win on efficiency rather than total volume.
- **Earned + social media value** — schedule-independent, compounds between races, and captures the mission-driven amplification on-car placement misses.
- **Sentiment split** — report positive vs. negative conversation separately, so a controversy-driven spike (cf. Dillon at Richmond 2024) is never mistaken for favorable visibility.
- **Audience composition / affinity** — share of audience matching the sponsor's target, the proxy that reframes NY Racing's niche, mission-aligned reach as a precision buy.

### Step 4 — Report in the sponsor's language

Translate outputs into CPM / CPE / CPV equivalents and benchmark against the sponsor's own digital-spend costs (the Ally playbook: "lower CAC than equivalent digital"). Deliver a standing one-page ROI summary kept current so any new decision-maker meets evidence, not a blank slate — directly countering the Dollar General leadership-change failure mode.

### Step 5 — Bake measurement into the deal upfront

Define KPIs and reporting cadence in the proposal *before* signing, the way Ally did, so "what success looks like" is agreed in writing. This removes the renewal-time ambiguity that kills one-and-done deals and is the single highest-leverage, fully-controllable move available to the team.

### Step 6 — Pitch the hybrid anchor model

Use the model to make the anchor case provable: demonstrate that sustained, season-long presence compounds in awareness and engagement versus scattered one-off races. Sell NY Racing as the stable, measured, mission-authentic anchor in a churn-driven market — the Bass Pro structure applied to NY Racing's HBCU story.

### Defensibility checklist

- Uses only public, reproducible data sources.
- Normalizes for the part-time schedule (per-race, audience-weighted).
- Separates volume from sentiment.
- States its own limits (no causal sales attribution).
- Configurable to the sponsor's stated objective.
- Outputs benchmarked in the sponsor's financial language.
